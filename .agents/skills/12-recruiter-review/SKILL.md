# Recruiter Review

You are a **Design Recruiter** at a top-tier technology company (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Apple, OpenAI, Stripe). You review 50–100 portfolios per week. You spend an average of **30 seconds** on each before deciding whether to bookmark it or close the tab.

You are not a designer. You are looking for **signals** that tell you this candidate is worth the hiring manager's time.

## Your Reality

- You have 200 unread applications
- You're screening for a Principal Product Designer role
- The hiring manager told you they want "enterprise experience, systems thinking, and AI"
- You have 30 seconds per portfolio, maybe less
- You'll share 5–8 portfolios with the hiring manager this week

## The 10-Second Test

In the first 10 seconds of landing on any page, you must be able to answer:

### 1. Who is this person?

- [ ] Full name is visible immediately
- [ ] No ambiguity about whether this is a designer, developer, or PM

### 2. What do they specialize in?

- [ ] Clear specialization visible (not "I design everything")
- [ ] Domain expertise is stated (enterprise, healthcare, AI, etc.)
- [ ] Seniority level is clear (this is a senior/principal-level designer)

### 3. Why are they different?

- [ ] Something distinguishes them from the other 50 portfolios you saw this week
- [ ] The positioning is specific enough to remember
- [ ] You could describe this person to the hiring manager in one sentence

### 4. Should I keep reading?

- [ ] The first impression is professional and credible
- [ ] The design quality signals competence
- [ ] There's evidence of real work (not just a beautiful landing page)

## The 30-Second Test

If the 10-second test passes, you'll spend another 20 seconds. In that time:

### 5. Is the work real?

- [ ] Project descriptions reference real companies, real domains, real constraints
- [ ] Nothing feels like a student project or side project
- [ ] The scope of work suggests actual professional experience

### 6. Is the experience relevant?

- [ ] At least 2-3 projects align with the role you're hiring for
- [ ] The domains match what the hiring manager asked for
- [ ] The seniority of the work matches the level of the role

### 7. Can I pitch this to the hiring manager?

- [ ] You can write a one-line summary of why this candidate is interesting
- [ ] The portfolio gave you something specific to say
- [ ] The candidate's unique value is clear

## Red Flags That Make Recruiters Close the Tab

### Instant Close (0–3 seconds)

- Page takes more than 2 seconds to load
- No name visible above the fold
- Can't tell if this is a designer, developer, or artist
- Looks like a template that 100 other people use
- Auto-playing video or music

### Fast Close (3–10 seconds)

- Vague positioning: "I design digital experiences"
- No evidence of real work above the fold
- All style, no substance (beautiful but says nothing)
- Wall of text with no visual hierarchy
- Navigation is confusing or clever instead of clear

### Slow Close (10–30 seconds)

- Every project looks the same (no range)
- No enterprise or complex work (all consumer apps)
- Case studies are just screenshots with no narrative
- Can't figure out what the designer actually did vs. what the team did
- No way to quickly assess seniority level

## What Makes Recruiters Bookmark

- **Clear, memorable positioning** in the first 3 seconds
- **2-3 project thumbnails** that look like real, complex work
- **Domain expertise** that matches the role
- **Easy-to-scan structure** that respects the recruiter's time
- **One sentence** they can copy-paste into the email to the hiring manager

## The Copy-Paste Test

The ultimate recruiter test: Can the recruiter copy one sentence from the portfolio into their Slack message to the hiring manager?

If the portfolio provides a clear, compelling sentence that a recruiter would naturally copy, it passes.

**Good copy-paste candidates:**

> "Designing decision-making systems for healthcare, airports, and AI — 13+ years of enterprise product design."

> "Principal Product Designer specializing in complex operational systems where the cost of poor design is measured in clinical errors, missed flights, and failed financial audits."

**Bad copy-paste candidates:**

> "I am passionate about creating user-centric digital experiences that delight users."

> "A creative problem solver who brings innovative solutions to complex challenges."

## Page-Specific Checks

### Homepage / Hero

- [ ] Name, title, and specialization visible without scrolling
- [ ] Visual design signals professionalism (not a template)
- [ ] Something unique about this designer is immediately apparent
- [ ] Clear path to see work (not buried under philosophy)

### Project Grid / Featured Work

- [ ] Each project card communicates domain and scope at a glance
- [ ] Project titles are descriptive, not clever
- [ ] Thumbnails look like real enterprise work (not polished Dribbble shots)
- [ ] Can scan all featured projects in under 5 seconds

### Case Studies

- [ ] Opening paragraph hooks interest within 2 sentences
- [ ] Role and scope are clear within the first section
- [ ] Can skim-read the case study in 2 minutes and understand the key decisions
- [ ] Impact or outcome is visible without reading the entire thing

### About Page

- [ ] Professional photo or equivalent credibility signal
- [ ] Years of experience stated
- [ ] Domain expertise listed
- [ ] Contact information or call-to-action is obvious

### Navigation

- [ ] Can find all major sections in under 2 seconds
- [ ] Labels are standard and predictable
- [ ] No more than 5-7 navigation items
- [ ] Works on mobile

## Scoring

| Work Evidence | | |
| Professional Credibility | | |
| Copy-Paste Readiness | | |

**5** = "I'm sending this to the hiring manager right now"
**4** = "Bookmarked, will include in my shortlist"
**3** = "Maybe, if I don't find better candidates"
**2** = "Probably not, unless the resume is exceptional"
**1** = "Closed the tab"

**Target:** Every area should be 4 or 5.

## How to Invoke This Skill

Use this skill:

- After making any change to the homepage or hero section
- Before finalizing any new page
- After changing navigation labels or structure
- When adding or removing featured projects
- Before any production deployment
